The World of 1155

Set the scene. 1155 AD. The Norse-Gaelic world of the western seaboards of Scotland and Ireland. The era of Somerled of Argyll — a man who straddled two worlds: the old Viking inheritance of his Norse-Gaelic ancestors and the rising tide of Norman feudal culture sweeping Britain. He fought not for Scandinavian kings but for his own sovereignty, and he did so with one foot in each world.

This kit carries that tension as its central theme. Every piece belongs to one of two narratives — the Old Ways (Norse-Gaelic ancestral tradition: the seax, the archaic antenna dagger, the self-bow) or the New Ways (Norman continental influence: the knightly arming sword). A warrior who carries both sends a message without speaking a word.

All bladed pieces sourced from Tod's Workshop / Tod Cutler (todsworkshop.com) — a British maker producing small-batch, hand-finished historical reproductions under direct maker oversight. Every piece in this kit shares the same quality standard, as if drawn from the same ancient blacksmith's forge.

Clothing & Dress

The garment is the first thing seen and the last thing remembered.

Primary Iconographic Source

The Lewis Chessmen (c. 1150–1200 AD, Outer Hebrides) — the definitive reference for Norse-Gaelic aristocratic dress of western Scotland at exactly this date. Kings, queens, and warders show fitted Norse kyrtill / kirtle construction: shaped upper body, gored flared skirt, fitted sleeves, and tablet-woven trim at cuffs, hem, and neckline. Every garment in this kit follows that silhouette.

Period Note — Sleeve Form

Simple fitted sleeves — NOT the wide baggy/hanging léine sleeves. That exaggerated form is post-1400 Late Medieval Irish, anachronistic for 1155. The Lewis Chessmen show fitted sleeves throughout.
