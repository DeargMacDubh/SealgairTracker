The World of 1155

Set the scene. 1155 AD. The Hiberno-Norse world of the western seaboards of Scotland and Ireland. The era of Somerled of Argyll — a man who straddled two worlds: the old Viking inheritance of his Norse-Gaelic ancestors and the rising tide of Norman feudal culture sweeping Britain. He fought not for Scandinavian kings but for his own sovereignty, and he did so with one foot in each world.

This kit carries that tension as its central theme. Every piece belongs to one of two narratives — the Old Ways (Norse-Gaelic ancestral tradition: the seax, the archaic antenna dagger, the self-bow) or the New Ways (Norman continental influence: the knightly arming sword). A warrior who carries both sends a message without speaking a word.

All bladed pieces sourced from Tod's Workshop / Tod Cutler (todsworkshop.com) — a British maker producing small-batch, hand-finished historical reproductions under direct maker oversight. Every piece in this kit shares the same quality standard, as if drawn from the same ancient blacksmith's forge.

Period Justification

The arming sword is the knightly weapon of Norman and continental Europe, adopted by ambitious Hiberno-Norse lords seeking legitimacy in the feudal order. By 1155, a sword of this type was a prestigious marker of warrior status across Britain and Ireland. It says: I have one foot in the world that is coming.

Period Justification

The seax is the ancestral Norse sidearm, carried by Gaelic-Norse warriors for centuries before 1155. By the 12th century it was a deliberately old weapon — worn by men who remembered where they came from. This piece, with its brass rivets and dot-circle handle motifs, speaks the visual language of antiquity without apology.
